# Critical Question Chain

Use these questions internally. The final answer should usually be compact rather than mechanically listing every question.

## 1. Core Structure

- What issue is being discussed?
- What conclusion does the author want the reader/listener to accept?
- What reasons are offered?
- Which reasons are central, and which are decorative or emotional support?

## 2. Terms And Meaning

- Which keywords are vague, ambiguous, or emotionally loaded?
- Does the author shift the meaning of a term during the argument?
- Are important terms defined by measurable criteria or only by impression?

## 3. Assumptions

- What factual assumptions must be true for the reasoning to work?
- What value assumptions are implied, especially when the text says "should", "must", "good", "bad", "worth", "harmful", or "necessary"?
- Would a reasonable person with different values reject the conclusion while accepting some reasons?

## 4. Reasoning Quality

- Do the reasons directly support the conclusion?
- Is the conclusion stronger than the reasons allow?
- Is there circular reasoning, false dilemma, straw man, slippery slope, ad hominem, appeal to popularity, appeal to questionable authority, or distraction?
- Is the argument confusing correlation with causation?

## 5. Evidence

- What type of evidence is used: data, research, example, anecdote, testimony, expert opinion, analogy, observation, intuition?
- Is the evidence sufficient, representative, current, relevant, and independently sourced?
- Are examples typical or cherry-picked?
- Are experts credible for this exact question, and are their claims represented accurately?

## 6. Data And Research

- Where did the data come from?
- What sample, method, baseline, comparison group, time period, and metric are used?
- Are averages, percentages, rankings, or visual framing potentially misleading?
- What data is omitted?

## 7. Causes And Alternatives

- Are there plausible alternative causes?
- Does the text rule out competing explanations?
- Is the claimed cause the only cause, a major cause, or one possible factor?
- Does "after this" get treated as "because of this"?

## 8. Missing Information

- What information would a fair reader need before accepting the conclusion?
- What counterevidence or tradeoff is not discussed?
- What negative consequences, costs, exceptions, or boundary conditions are missing?

## 9. Opposing View

- What is the strongest opposing argument?
- Does the author address a real opponent or a weakened version?
- What would need to be added to make the argument persuasive to a skeptical but fair reader?

## 10. Reasonable Conclusions

- What conclusions are justified by the current reasons?
- What weaker or more conditional conclusion would be more defensible?
- Under what conditions would the original conclusion become stronger?
